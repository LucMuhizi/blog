## The hours already taken

This series has named the care tax, the invisible extraction that sustains households and economies. Now we ask: what does that extraction do to a day, to a body, to a life? This is the time tax.

## She wakes before dawn

She wakes at 4am. Not by choice. By necessity. The water queue starts before sunrise. If she is not there, there is no water for cooking, washing, drinking. She queues. She carries twenty litres home. Her back already aches.

The market opens at 6am. She has to be early. Prices change with the hour. She negotiates, recalls yesterday's price, walks five kilometres. She buys flour, soap, fuel. Each step is a calculation. By 9am, she has worked three hours before any of the rest of the day has begun.

## What the time tax pays for

That time she carried water, queued, walked, negotiated, carried again, and unloaded at the door: it pays for the basic right to live. It pays for the household to function. It pays for the children to be fed and schooled. It pays for the economy that, on paper, treats her as a worker and, in practice, treats her as free.

The time tax is the most honest line in any ledger. Every other line has a price tag she can point to. The time tax has only the body that wakes and the body that returns.

## A budget of hours

What does extraction look like when measured in hours? Across a day, a woman averages 4.6 more hours of unpaid work than a man in the same household. Across a week, that is more than a full working day given away without pay. Across a year, more than ten working weeks. Across a decade, almost a full calendar year of life absorbed by the system before she ever keeps it.

If the care tax is the invisible budget line, the time tax is the visible one. The hours are right there, every day, in the body. The question isn't whether the time tax exists. The question is who is asked to pay it, who decides, and how it is named.

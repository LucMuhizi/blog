## Consumption, survival, and the taxation of everyday life

She buys food. VAT is already embedded in its price. She buys fuel. Excise is already embedded in its price. She buys soap, maize flour, school supplies. Tariffs are already embedded in their prices.

There is no moment before tax. There is no space outside it. There is no way around survival costs that are already structured by fiscal design. Consumption is not where taxation begins. It is where taxation becomes impossible to ignore.

## The tax on being alive

Every transaction in the household economy is a tax event. To cook, she pays a tax on the gas, on the maize, on the salt. To school a child, she pays a tax on the uniform, on the book, on the registration. To move through the city, she pays a tax on the ticket, on the fuel, on the road. Even to stay still — to sit at home, to fix the roof — there is a tax embedded in the cement, the nail, the timber.

This is the tax you cannot escape. It is not the tax of the rich, paid on capital gains and inheritance. It is the tax of survival, paid every day by the people who keep households, economies, and infrastructure running.

## A framework, not a complaint

Naming this tax is not a complaint. It is the start of a framework: feminism, gender and power, lived through fiscal structure. If care is the invisible labour that holds up the household, then tax is the fiscal structure that takes the household's income as a given. Both are extracted. Both are seldom named. Both are also the places where structural equity must be designed, because they are the places where structural inequity is currently built.
